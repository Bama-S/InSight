Derived words and morphology

We also have to take a close look at the words themselves. The fact that words are of two types, roots and derived words was discovered by ancient Indian grammarians around fifth century B.C. Derived words are words where the roots are joined with affixes which in turn change the function of the root in some way or the other. The rules governing this joining of roots with affixes to form derived roots are called morphological rules.

Let us take a look at some morphological rules in English.

Rule 1: One rule gives the derivation of the plural form, from the root word (singular). This states that normally 's' is added to root word to get the derivate.
       
                Ex:       Girl    ---> girls

But this is not the rule to apply for all cases.

Rule 1a:  Another rule states that if the word ends in 'ife', the  plural is got by replacing 'ife'  by  'ives'.

              Ex :   Life ------> lives

Rule 1b:  Another rule states that same words ending in 'y' becomes plural by removing  the 'y' and adding 'ies' as in 

                       fly  ------> flies.

But some words ending  in 'y' just take an 's' as in

                       boy  ------> boys

Another interesting factor about number rules is that in  case of verbs, the root is normally plural, while the conversion is given as the singular.  This is the converse of nouns.

                Ex:   The man flies.
                         The men fly.

Another set of rules concerning verbs is the conversion of root verb (in present tense) to past or infinitive.

                Ex:   Play, played, playing.

There are other mappings which have verbs joining with aspect and modality words to form a verb group. (*)
Thus a verb is normally associated with Tense, Aspect and Modality or TAM indicators.

Thus, in English, as we have seen, affixes are added to words  to convey number and gender.  Affixes also sometimes convey the opposite.

                 Ex:    understood -  misunderstood
                           interested -  uninterested

So this category of rules that we have seen are morphological rules,  i.e., rules of how affixes combine with roots to convey number, gender and in some cases, opposite meaning. Now you may be wondering why we need to have this separation between roots and derivatives (i.e. words obtained by joining words with affixes).  It is the roots that are maintained in the dictionary. Imagine if we had to maintain a dictionary which had roots and all its derivatives - what a large one would it be!
