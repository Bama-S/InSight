Introduction

Folks, imagine yourself in some Lingualand where the Lingua language that's spoken doesn't have a well-defined grammar. Say, a particular sentence means different things to different people. Mind you, the individual words in the sentence do not have varying meanings. It's just the difference in the interpretation of the grouping of the words. Obviously, that's unimaginable!

Now the question is how do you think we humans manage to communicate with each other through speech or writing? On a bit of thinking it must become clear to you that it's because of the conventions that we adhere to, that we are able to communicate. These conventions vary from culture to culture and language to language. These conventions are formalized as rules which again vary from language to language. In our early years we learn language primarily by listening to and observing people around us 'communicate'! And then more formally we were taught these conventions as Grammar in school. So what's Grammar?

Grammar is a category of rules that any language follows. It is something that standardizes the way we communicate. We'll start by looking  at the English language and then move on to Indian languages. In Indian languages, some of the well known works that formalize these language are in fact considered classics. We will be looking at the rules of Sanskrit language through PaaNini's work and those of Tamil language through Tholkaappiyam. This we'll do later and also make comparisons between the nature of English, Hindi and Tamil languages so as to understand these rules or conventions better.
