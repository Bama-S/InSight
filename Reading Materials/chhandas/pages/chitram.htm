You might have read about alliteration (occurrence of same sound in two or more words in succession to produce a musical effect) and onomatopoeia (usage of words so as to convey the mood). But what's pictorial representation of verses? Sounds strange, doesn't it? Well, let's find out. In Sanskrit, it is possible to compose a verse so that it follows a geometric pattern. Such a pattern used to form the verse is called a 'bandham'.

Look at the following figure. Can you make out what it is ?

Well, probably you can't. Let's try again. Consider this shloka :

पापाकूपारपालीपा त्रिपादीपादपादपा |
कृपारूपा जपालापा स्वापा मापानृपाधिपा ||
paapaa koopaarapaaleepaa tripaadeepaadapaadapaa
krpaa roopaa japaalaapaa svaapaamaapaannrpaadhipaa


Meaning: The protector of Lord's feet, destroyer of all sins,
embodiment of the Lord's mercy, which can be attained easily,
which is higher than the kings, that paaduka (sandal of the lord) protects me.

  

Now try to connect the figure and the verse. You may have found out the pattern by now. The letter at the centre appears as every alternate letter. The numbers near the letters give the position of those letters in the verse.

For example, the first letter is 'paa', second one is 'paa' (at the center), third is 'koo', fourth is 'paa' (at the center) and so on. This pattern is called padma bandham, as it can be represented in the form of a lotus.

Another pattern which we shall look into is called gomootrikaa bandham. A shlokaa written using this pattern can be read in two different ways. One, as usual, and the other as : first letter of the first half, second letter of the second half, third letter of the first, fourth of the second, and so on. The shlokaa is given below :

सुरासुरार्चिता धन्या तुंग मंगल पालिका।
चराचराश्रिता मान्या रंग पुंगव पादुका।।
suraa suraarchitaa dhanya tunga mangala paalikaa
charaa charaashritaa maanyaa ranga pungava paadukaa


Meaning: The paadukaa (sandal) of shree Ranganaatha, endowed
_ with both moving and stationary wealths,
_ worshipped by both devaas and asuraas, is the protection for
_ all auspicious things.
  

Here is how the verse is represented pictorially :

Follow the blue line, and you get the first half of the shlokaa. Similarly, read along the red line, and you get the second half.

Another kind is paadaanuloma pratilomam. The design behind this shlokaa is simple : the first quarter of the verse, when read backwards, gives the second. Similarly, the third quarter of the verse, read backwards, gives the fourth :

रामपादगता भासा साभातागदपामरा।
कादुपानञ्चकासह्या ह्यासकाञ्चन पादुका।।
raamapaada gataa bhaasa saabhaata gadapaamaraa
kaadupaanancha kaa sahyaa hyaasakaanchana paadukaa


Meaning: The paadukaa (sandal) which is very illuminous,
protector of Brahma and Shiva, which cannot be tolerated
by those who don't worship it, and which adorned the feet
of Lord Raama, wasn't it the protector of the kingdom
(when Raama lived in the forest)?
  

This is the shlokaa. Read along the direction of the arrows. Read the first quarter from left to right, and the second from right to left. Similarly, read the third from left to right, and the fourth from right to left.

 

 

 

Let's take another pattern. Look at the figure below and try to form the verse :

If you can't, look at the verse below, and try to find the connection.

सराघवा श्रुतौ धृष्टा पादुका सनृपासना।
सलाघवा गतौ श्लिष्टा स्वादुर्मे सदुपासना।।
saraaghava shrtau drShTaa paadukaa sanrpaasanaa
saraaghava gatau shliShTaa svaadurme sadupaasanaa

Meaning: The paadukaa (sandal) - which was held in esteem
in the Vedaas, which sat in the throne along with Shree
Raama, which is suitable for worship, which is constantly
in touch with the Lord's feet - is sweet to me.

  

The verse is read as follows : the first 4 letters, 'saraaghavaa' are common to both the halves of the verse. Similarly, the last 3 letters, 'paasanaa' are common to both halves. In between, we read the verse like this : the letters within the ellipses are common to both halves, but those letters divided by the line are not.The first half of the verse is constructed by the letters above the line and those inside the ellipse, read alternately. Similarly, the lower half includes the letters below the line and those inside the ellipses. This is somewhat similar to gomootrikaa bandham. This kind of arrangement is called shara bandham. 'shara' in Sanskrit means arrow, and since the pattern has the shape of an arrow head, it got this name.

The above verses are taken from a work called 'paadukaa sahasram'( by Sri Vedaanta Deshika, an exponent of VaiShNava philosophy). This work contains many more verses like these, and they are much more intricate in their construction.

These verses are presented to you just to hint at the level of sophistication attained by our poets in the construction of poems a long time ago.
