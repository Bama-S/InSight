Nyaaya divides inference into two forms.

1. Svaartha (inference for oneself).

2. Paraartha (inference for others).

The former is a psychological process using which one infers for oneself. The latter is a reasoning that has to be done in language, to convince others. It is a formal procedure through which one puts forth one's process of reasoning in a cogent manner to others.  

Svaartha is the cause of one's own inferential knowledge. Going back to the previous example of smoke and mountain, having repeatedly seen the vyaapti of fire with smoke in the fireplace etc.,  one arrives at the universal generalization - 'wherever there is smoke there is fire'. Then, when he goes near a mountain and sees smoke there, there dawns in him the knowledge that the mountain has smoke pervaded by fire. As already mentioned, this is known as paraamarsha. From paraamarsha, the knowledge, "the mountain has fire in it" arises. This process is svaartha anumaanam i.e., inference for oneself.

But, this isn't enough when one has to convince others of his inference. So, he employs a sound  reasoning called paraartha anumaanam to make others understand the same. The five members of this reasoning are:

1. Pratijnaa - States the proposition which is to be proved.

2. Hetu - States the reason for the establishment of the proposition.

3. Udaaharana - Gives the vyaapti along with an example.

4. Upanaya - Applies vyaapti to the present case.

5. Nigamana - Gives the conclusion drawn from the preceding propositions.

Let us see what these five members are in case of our example of mountain and smoke.

1. This mountain  has fire. (pratijnaa)

2. Because it has smoke. (hetu)

3. Whatever has smoke has fire, eg., hearth. (udaaharana)

4. This hill has smoke (which is invariably associated with fire). (upanaya)

5. Therefore, this hill has fire. (nigamana)

Inference, as we know generally, is a deduction process but nyaaya has rightly regarded inference as a combination of two inseparably related processes viz., deduction (the process of infering a particular instance from a general rule) and induction (the process of infering the general rule from particular instances).Why should it be so? Deduction starts with a universal major premise and tries to prove a particular instance. Actually, the universal premise itself is the result of  induction based on repeatedly observed instances. Hence, nyaaya combines both induction (for recognizing vyaapti) and deduction (for infering the thing to be proved) into the process of inference.
