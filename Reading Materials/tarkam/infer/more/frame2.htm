Vyaapti, as we have studied, forms the nerve of inference. Hence, the recognition of vyaapti is very important for sound inference. Usually, we take vyaapti to be a generalization of repeated instances of the same kind. We see an event occurring repeatedly due to the same reason or two things being co-existent wherever we see them. After we are convinced of their invariable association, we identify the vyaapti. But we must be very careful in generalization. Care must be taken to consider all possible instances and exceptions. Only when a generalization is free of exceptions and suspicions, can we use it as vyaapti in inference. 

An example will make things clear:

One may observe a hundred times that earthly substances can be scratched by metals. Still, in things like diamond, a deviation to the above rule is observed (i.e., though diamond is a particular modification of earth, no scratches can be caused by any metal on it. Only a diamond can scratch another). Thus, vyaapti is to be interpreted as the knowledge of co-existence free from fallacious knowledge (fallacious knowledge can be either certainty of a contrary fact or mere suspicion).

Not only have our ancestors given a way for using induction to identify vyaapti but also classified it into three categories.They are:

1. Anvayavyatireki  vyaapti (positive-negative vyaapti)  - Here, the hetu is both positively  and negatively related to the saadhya. That is, wherever hetu is found, saadhya is found. Wherever saadhya is not found, hetu is not found. One can give examples both for the former and the latter case.

Example:

Positive part:

Wherever there is smoke, there is fire. This is seen in the case of hearth.

Here, the hetu (smoke) is positively related to the saadhya (fire). We can find an example for such a relation. Hearth is such an example.

Negative part:

Wherever there is no fire, there is no smoke. This is seen in the case of a lake.


Here, the hetu (smoke) is negatively  related to the saadhya (fire). We can find an example for such a relation too. Lake is such an example.

 

2. Kevalaanvayi vyaapti (purely positive vyaapti) - The hetu is always positively related to the saadhya. That is, wherever hetu is found, saadhya is also found. But we can't find any example where saadhya is not found. So, we can't   assert that "wherever saadhya is not found, hetu is not found".

Example:

Consider the argument "A jar is nameable because it is knowable like a pot". Sound's confusing doesn't it !   Actually, something is called 'knowable' if  it can be perceived. Something is 'nameable' when we can attribute a name to it.

Here, the vyaapti is " That which is knowable is also nameable". The hetu is the 'knowable' nature of the jar. Saadhya is the 'nameability' of the jar. There is a purely positive relation between the hetu and the saadhya. However, we can't come up with any example indicating the negative relation between the hetu and saadhya. That is, we can't say that "That which is not nameable is not knowable" because, everything in this world is nameable.

3. Kevalavyatireki vyaapti (purely negative vyaapti) - The hetu is always negatively related to the saadhya. That is, wherever saadhya is not found, hetu is not found. But we can't find any instance where hetu is found. So we cannot say that "wherever hetu is found, saadhya is also found".

Example:

Consider the argument "Ram can't fly because he is a human being who doesn't have wings"  Here, the vyaapti is "Human beings who don't have wings, can't fly". The relation between the hetu (Ram is a human being without wings) and the saadhya (Ram can't fly) is purely negative. That is, we can't say "If human beings can fly, they'll have wings" because, no human being can fly.

We know what paksha is. It is the place where the saadhya is suspected. Two more terms associated with vyaapti and paksha are 'sapaksha' and 'vipaksha'.  We'll look at them.

In case of our mountain example, the mountain was the paksha. We cited the example of a hearth where the vyaapti between smoke and fire is found. This is a positive instance. That is, there is certainty of the saadhya (fire) and also the hetu (smoke). Hence, hearth is called 'sapaksha' or the positive instance. Similarly, in case of a lake, we see the absence of fire and hence the absence of smoke. Hence, the lake is called the negative instance or the vipaksha. Here, we're certain that there is no fire. Hence, 'vipaksha' is one where the absence of the saadhya is certain.
