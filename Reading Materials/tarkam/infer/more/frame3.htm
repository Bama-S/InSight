Following are some examples for paraartha anumaanam.

1) "It's going to rain because there are dark clouds looming over."

1. We anticipate rain in the city.(pratijnaa)
2. Because there are dark clouds. (hetu)
3. Wherever there are dark clouds there will be rain.(example is trivial )
4. Dark clouds over a place are invariably associated with future rain in that place. (upanaya)
5. Therefore we anticipate rain in the city. (nigamana)

In the above example, we infer the unperceived effect from a perceived cause i.e., we infer future rain from dark clouds in the sky.

2) "It has rained because the water of the river is swift, muddy and flooded."

1. We guess there was rain in the village (pratijnaa).
2. Because we observe the swift, muddy, flooded water of a river (hetu).
3. Whenever swift muddy flooding is observed in a river there should have been rain.
4. Swift muddy flooding (which is invariably associated with past rain) is observed in the village (upanaya).
5. Therefore we guess there was rain in the village (nigamana).

In the above example, we infer the unperceived cause from the perceived effect. Past rain is the unperceived cause and the flooded  river water is the perceived effect.

3) "Cows have cloven hoofs because they have horns".

1. Cows have cloven hoofs. (pratijnaa)
2. Because they have horns. (hetu)
3. Animals that have horns, have cloven hoofs because of co-existence of horns and cloven hoofs. (e.g. goat)
4. Cows have horns which are invariably co-existent with cloven hoofs. (upanaya)
5. Therefore, cows have cloven hoofs. (nigamana)

In the above example, unlike the first two, there is no cause-effect relationship. The relationship between hetu and saadhya is mere invariable co-existence.

4) Many of you might have played the well-known game "MineSweeper". A lot of inference is involved in this game. Consider a snapshot from the game, which looks something like:

 

About the game:

You have a grid containing cells as shown. Cells may or may not have a mine. If they don't have a mine, then they contain a number. This number corresponds to the number of immediate surrounding cells with a mine. At the start of the game all the cells are unrevealed.  So, we don't know about any cell. Cells are revealed by clicking on them. If we click on a mine, the mine blasts and we lose. If the cell we click on is not a mine, it is revealed and shows out a number which corresponds to the no. of surrounding mines. The object of the game is to find all the mines on the playing field.

Here, in the grid shown above, we have cells which aren't numbered and are blank. Assume that they are cells without any mines. Other cells which are marked in the figure are :

M cells                    -         A mine is present.
Grey  cells               -        Cell which is not revealed.
Numbered cells        -       The number indicates the number of mines around that cell.                                   (Ranges from 1 to  8). The cell itself is free of mines.
Blank cells               -       cells without any mines.


Here, for simplicity, let the columns be referred by numbers while the rows by alphabets. The cells are then named by their corresponding row name and column name.

Let us find the hidden mines in the above board. It involves inference. We'll find the hidden mines and indicate the members of paraartha anumaanam in the process. The last two members of the paraartha anumaanam viz., upanaya and nigamana are left out since they are easily found given the first three members.

Stage 1:-

1. Cell C2 or C3 should have a mine. (pratijnaa)
2. Cell B2 is numbered 3. 6 cells surrounding B2 are revealed. Among them two cells ( B1, C1) have mines. (hetu)
3. A cell X is numbered n, and that cell has m cells surrounding it. Out of that m cells r cells are revealed and have k mines in them (We know the exact position of the mines). Then among the remaining m-r cells surrounding X, n-k cells have mine.

(eg: If a cell is numbered 1 (n), and that cell have 8 (m) cells surrounding it, out of which 3 (r) cells are revealed and have 1 (k) mine in them, then among the remaining (8-3=5) cells, 0 cells have mine.) (udaaharana)

Therefore, C2 or C3 have a mine.

Stage 2:

1.  Cell   C4  is    free. (pratijnaa)
2.  Cell   B3  is numbered one. There are 8 cells surrounding it.
      From Stage 1 : C2 or C3  have a mine.(hetu)
3.  A cell X is numbered n, and that cell has m cells surrounding it. We also know that k mines are present in any of  the r cells around it (even if we are'nt aware of the exact position of the mines). Then, among the remaining m-r cells surrounding X, n-k cells have mine.

(eg.  A cell is numbered 2. There are 8 cells surrounding it. Out of that we know that 1 mine  is present in any of the 5 cells. Then, among the remaining (8-5 = 3)  any one should have a mine.) (udaaharana)

Thus, C4 is free.

The rest of the process is very simple by considering cell B4  and marking the mines around it. Try putting down the final inference as five members of reasoning.

The final answer is :

            Cell C3 and C5 have a mine each.
