Cognition

When we dealt with guNa earlier,  we saw that cognition could be divided into remembrance and apprehension.

Apprehension can be valid or invalid.   This apprehension is the basic grounding for inferencing.
As seen earlier valid apprehension comes from

what one perceives
what one infers
one's analytical knowledge
verbal testimony.

Perception is apprehension by contact of an organ with the object, i.e. what one hears, sees, feels,etc
For example, when we touch fire, we feel  its heat.

Inferencing is correlation of what one knows or perceives.
For example, when the mother hears her infant crying, she can infer  that baby is hungry or that it wants to sleep.

Analytical knowledge primarily deals with reasoning the cause of events and their actions.  It is inborn in every life form.
Primarily it is a tool or aid that helps in inferencing.
We carry an umbrella when it is cloudy.  We do this because we know that clouds cause rain, and their presence indicate a possibility of rain.

Verbal testimony is what one hears from a reliable source (e.g. a teacher) and accepts without question. 
For example, when we hear that the number of stars in the sky are practically infinite, we accept it without testing its validity.

Cause and Effects

Inferencing is analysing the causes and effects of what one perceives, thereby leading to a conclusion.  Whenever there is an effect, there should be a cause and vice-versa.  In fact it can be said that, there exists an inherent relationship between the cause and the effect. Moreover cause invariably precedes effect.
The rotation of earth around its axis is the cause of day and night. (Right cause)
But one shouldn't say, day  and night is caused due to the revolution of sun around the earth, as the cause is invalid.

During the discussion about various causes and their effects, we'll take the example of the weaving of the cloth.

Causes can be of 2 types: the general or the specific.

That which is the cause of every effect is called the general cause. It
Time, space and destiny are general causes.

That cause which is the specific to an effect is the particular cause.
For example, yarn in the production of cloth is the special cause.

Particular causes further can be divided into:

Inherent cause
Non-inherent cause
Instrumental cause

Inherent cause or primal cause is the one without which the effect is impossible.
e.g. Yarn is the inherent cause of the cloth.

Non-inherent cause is that, which is not inherent to the effect but is inherent in the primal cause.
e.g. The color of yarn is the non-inherent cause of the color of the cloth.  It is inherent in the yarn,  but is non-inherent in the cloth.  This color is the cause of coloring in the yarn, but it only incidental to the color of the cloth.

The Instrumental cause is the device or tool that caused the effect. It cannot independently cause an effect. It invariably complements the above 2 causes.
In the above example, the loom is the instrumental cause for the production of cloth(effect).

Special causes are those without which the effect is impossible.  They are the one and only cause of the event.
For example, when an arrow hits the bird, it brings about the death of the bird instantaneously.   Here the arrow is the special cause as it alone brings about the death of the bird, by its own activity of striking the bird.
