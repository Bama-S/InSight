अभाव

Absence

The final category is negation or Abhaava  (pronounced as a-bhaa-va)

While others talk about existence of one or more characteristics abhaava talks about their non-existence.
The first six categories are positive while abhava is negative.
Unlike the others which are regarded as absolute, abaava is relative in its conception.

Absence of an object is different from the knowledge of its absence.
It is this knowledge of the absence that is referred to as abhaavam.

The Vaishesika, being a realist, says that if one does not know that an object exists it doesn't mean that the object doesn't exist.  In other words the object exists, whether or not one knows about it.

For example, the electron existed even before its discovery.  Only we didn't know about its existence.

Similarly, Knowledge of negation is different from the thing negated.
i.e. the thing negated is a substance or its quality but the knowledge of such negation is abhaavam.

Non-existence is of four kinds:

1) Antecedent non-existence(praagabhaava प्रागभाव )

Praagabhaava is the non-existence of a thing before production.  Praagabhaava has no beginning but it has an end.
For example, a pot does not exist before its production.  We can talk about the existence of the pot only after it has been created.
Here beginning and end refers to the non-existence(abhaava) of the pot and not the pot  itself. This negation ends when the pot is produced.
If praagabhaava is denied then existence of things would become beginningless.

2) Subsequent non-existence(pradhvamsaabhaava प्रध्वंसाभाव)

Pradhvamsaabhaava is the non-existence of a thing after its destruction. Pradhvamsaabhaava has a beginning but has no end.
For example, e.g. the pot does not exist after its destruction.
pradhvamsaabhaava begins when the thing is destroyed and has no end since the same thing cannot be produced again.
If pradhvamsaabhaava is denied then things become everlasting.

3) Absolute non-existence(atyantaabhaava अत्यन्ताभाव )

Atyantaabhaava is the absence of things in the past, the present and the future. It is both beginningless and endless. How can a thing be always absent? We'll explain this with an example. The absence of a pot in a room is an example of Atyantaabhaava. Don't be baffled by this statement. Let's make it a bit easy. In the room, the pot was never there in the past and it will not be there in the future. But what if some one places a pot there? When the pot is placed, it hides the Atyantaabhaava. Thus Atyantaabhaava has neither a beginning nor an end. But why should you have a such a concept? If atyantaabhaava is denied then things would exist always and everywhere.

4) mutual non-existence(anyonyaabhaava अन्योन्याभाव)

Anyonyaabhaava is the non-existence of a thing in presence of another. It is both beginningless and endless. If anyonaabhaava is denied then all things would become indistinguishable.
e.g.Consider 2 objects - say a pot and a cloth. There is the absence of the pot in the cloth and similarly, the absence of the cloth in the pot. Thus there is an anyonyaabhaava (mutual non-existence) between the cloth and the pot.
