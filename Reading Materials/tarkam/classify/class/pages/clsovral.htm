Classification system followed in Tarka Shaastram

We now take a look at a classification system used in the science of Nyaya-Shastra. What is unique and striking about this system is that it attempts to classify everything - from the atom to the universe - within a single grand scheme. It is the universality of this scheme that makes it special. Sounds interesting! Well, let's take a look at it. We focus on the vaisheshika school of thought.

The Vaiseshika system divides things into 7 broad categories or padharthaas. They are :

  1. Substance
  2. Quality
  3. Action
  4. Generality
  5. Particularity
  6. Inherence
  7. Absence

Let us take a look at each of them in turn.

Substance

Substance is the matter that things are made of.  It immediately follows that most of the substances can be perceived by one's senses.
For example,
Light can be seen, Food can be smelt and tasted, Breeze can be felt,  Thunder  can be heard.
Here light, food, breeze and thunder are substances.

Quality

Quality is that which is possessed by a substance.  It helps one to distinguish one substance from another. They define the characteristics of objects. 
It can be likened to the adjective in a sentence.
For example,
when we talk about sweet  food, hot  water, etc
the words sweet and hot characterise the substances food and water.

Action

Actions are those events which are performed on or by the substances. They cannot occur in the absence of a substance.
For example,
when a person eats   food, the process of eating is referred to as action.
Similarly, a horse running, a child crying are all actions.

Generality

Generality is that property which is common to several objects.  It resides in more than one substance or rather a group of substances.  It can be referred to as a group characteristic.
For example,
tail is common to almost all animal species.

Particularity

Particularity is the distinguishing feature of an substance.  It constitutes the individuality of objects. It lays an emphasis on the uniqueness of individual or a group of objects.
For example,
When we talk about groups, we say that fins are unique to fishes (as a group).
When we consider substances individually, we say no two snow flakes are alike.

Inherence

Inherence is that inseparable relationship, which exists between the substance and its quality. One of these cannot exist without the other.
For example,
smell is inherent in a rose.

Absence

Absence is non-existence of one or more of the above characteristics.  It can be expressed using negative prefixes like   dis- , un- , in- , mis- ,etc.
For example,
dis-proportion refers to absence of proportion;
darkness is absence of light.

The 7 categories that have been mentioned here are considered to be comprehensive. The fact that this is all-encompassing is supported by rigorous arguments. However it is easier to understand them using examples.
