MOTION OF THE PLANETS:

  Is there any significant difference between the motion of the planets and the motion of the sun and the moon ? Yes, there is. While the sun and the moon always move towards east, the planets sometimes move towards the west.

Long ago, our forefathers discovered that some planets move in a westward direction. Planets which move in westward direction are said to have a retrograde (backward) motion. And planets moving in eastward direction are said to have direct motion.

When the motion of the planet is direct, it is 'margi' and when the motion is retrograde, it is 'vakri'.

The retrograde motion of the planets have puzzled astronomers for centuries. It is very difficult to understand if we consider the geocentric theory. But when we consider that all the planets move around the sun it is easy to explain this peculiar motion of the planets. Since earth is also a planet, it is also assumed to move around the sun.

Suppose that a man observes a fixed star, say Ashwini and measures the angle of inclination with a planet, say Saturn (mind you, this fellow is very slow). Assume that the observer again measures the angle between the star and Saturn in a few months (it would have hardly moved by that time). If it so happens that this measured angle is smaller than the previously measured angle, the planet observed would appear to have moved in the westward direction with respect to the star. This apparent motion of the planet in the opposite direction is called 'retrograde motion'. The retrograde motion can be observed only at certain periods of time, which depends on the planet being observed.

For example, the planet Mars had a retrograde or westward motion (from October 20th 1990 to January 1st 1991). Saturn had a retrograde motion from May 4th 1990 to September 23rd 1990.
